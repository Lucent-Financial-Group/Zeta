# B-0384 — Formalizability assessment: calculation problem → information-theoretic complexity bound

## What

Assess whether the Misesian calculation-problem primitive admits
quant-grade mathematical formalization as an information-theoretic
complexity bound. Produce a committed research note at
`docs/aurora/YYYY-MM-DD-primitive-calculation-problem.md`.

## The Austrian primitive

Mises 1920 ("Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth"):
rational economic calculation requires market prices generated by
private property + voluntary exchange. A central planner cannot
replicate market prices because the information needed is:
(a) distributed across millions of actors,
(b) tacit / non-articulable in many cases (Hayek 1945 knowledge
problem extension),
(c) created *through* the market process itself, not pre-existing
it (prices as discovery mechanism, not just signals of fixed data).

The calculation-problem is not merely "too many equations to solve"
(the socialist planners had this objection and answered it with
linear programming). It is a deeper claim: the data the equations
need to solve *do not exist* in any form that permits aggregation
prior to the market process.

## The quant/CS-side analogs

The calculation-problem is structurally analogous to several
established results in computer science and information theory:

- **Hayek's information problem** → distributed systems /
  Byzantine consensus: information that cannot be centralized
  without loss is precisely the subject of distributed algorithms
  and impossibility results (Fischer-Lynch-Paterson, CAP theorem)
- **Price as signal** → sufficient-statistic literature: when is
  a price a sufficient statistic for the underlying private
  information? (Grossman-Stiglitz 1980 paradox; market
  microstructure literature)
- **Complexity bounds** → computational complexity: can "the
  planner cannot compute" be stated as a formal complexity
  result (NP-hard, PSPACE-hard, or undecidable)?
- **Tacit knowledge** → Kolmogorov complexity: can the Hayekian
  tacit-knowledge claim be stated as "the description length of
  the information needed exceeds any feasible transmission
  channel"?

## Research questions

1. Does the calculation-problem reduce to a known computational
   complexity result, or does it require a new formal framework?
   (The "planning is NP-hard" claim has been made informally;
   has it been proven rigorously?)

2. Can the Hayekian extension (knowledge problem: prices as
   discovery, not signals) be stated as a formal claim about
   emergence of information from market processes? What
   mathematical theory of emergence applies?

3. What is the relationship between the Grossman-Stiglitz paradox
   (if markets fully reveal private information, no one has
   incentive to acquire it) and the Austrian claim that market
   prices are epistemically superior to central planning?

4. The calculation problem is arguably the Austrian primitive
   MOST amenable to formal computer-science treatment because
   it is already stated in computational/information terms.
   Does existing distributed systems literature (consensus
   protocols, information aggregation mechanisms) constitute
   partial formalization, or does it miss the Austrian point?
