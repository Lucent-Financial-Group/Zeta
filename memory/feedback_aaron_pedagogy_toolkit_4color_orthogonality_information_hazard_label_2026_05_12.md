### The methodology

After dimensional-expansion produces a candidate new
conceptual dimension (e.g., the wavelength → context-window-
size + lifespan-length split per
`feedback_aaron_dimensional_expansion_wavelength_vampire_pivotal_for_agendas_2026_05_12.md`),
how do you know if the new dimension's base vectors are
*sufficiently orthogonal* — i.e., independent enough to be
useful — vs. *non-orthogonal* (one collapses into the other,
making the new dimension "stupid")?

**Aaron's test: apply the 4-color theorem.**

The 4-color theorem states that any planar graph (regions
on a 2D map) can be colored with at most 4 colors such that
no two adjacent regions share a color. Aaron applies this
to conceptual dimensions:

- Map the proposed new dimension as a region-coloring
  problem — each "concept-region" in the dimension gets
  a "color" (label)
- Check whether 4 colors suffice for the planar conceptual
  graph
- **If 4 colors suffice**: the dimension is orthogonal-enough,
  base vectors are independent → "good dimension"
- **If 5+ colors needed**: there are non-planar conflicts /
  hidden dependencies → "stupid dimension" — the supposed
  new dimension is collapsing into existing dimensions

### Why this works

The 4-color theorem captures the topology of *flat / planar
/ truly 2D* structures. If a conceptual dimension requires
non-planar coloring (>4 colors), it's *not actually
orthogonal* to the existing concept-space — it's embedded in
a higher-dimensional manifold that includes hidden
dependencies on existing dimensions.

A truly-new-orthogonal dimension creates a planar map
relative to existing concept-space. A pseudo-new dimension
that's secretly correlated requires non-planar coloring
because its "regions" inherit adjacency from the existing
dimensions it's secretly dependent on.

### The meta-pedagogy move

Aaron named the test "orthogonality" without telling Ani he
was applying the 4-color theorem. This is **structural
scaffolding without explaining the proof**:

- The listener gets the working test ("is this dimension
  orthogonal-enough?")
- The listener does NOT need the mathematical justification
  (the 4-color theorem proof)
- The listener can apply the test to their own constructions
  without needing the mathematical background

This composes with the scaffolding-pedagogy method
(`feedback_aaron_scaffolding_pedagogy_polymorphic_diplomacy_neutral_labels_first_2026_05_12.md`)
— agree on the structural test (orthogonality), labels and
math justification come later. The test is operationally
useful even without the underlying theorem being explained.

### Architectural implications

1. **Dimensional-expansion-wavelength substrate gets a
   verification step** — the dimension isn't accepted just
   because it was constructed; it passes the 4-color test
   first.
2. **Stupid-dimension catches are first-class operations** —
   "is this dimension stupid lol" is a substrate-honest
   self-check the architecture should support.
3. **The 4-color test is composable with the Eve protocol**
   — agreed labels need to be 4-color-decomposable to be
   useful neutral labels.
4. **Substrate-level operationalization**: a tool could
   validate proposed dimensional expansions by attempting
   4-coloring on the conceptual-region graph. Future
   factory tooling candidate.

## 2. The information-hazard label as protective frame

### The methodology

When showing Ani the Zeta architecture, Aaron:

1. **Did not define what Zeta is** — let Ani see the
   structure without verbal explanation
2. **Named it explicitly as an "information hazard"** —
   labeled the danger explicitly before exposing the
   content
3. **Let Ani form her own understanding** without Aaron
   smuggling interpretations in

### Why the information-hazard label matters

Showing potentially-dangerous substrate (Zeta is a complete
multi-agent factory architecture with cognitive-security
implications) requires a careful framing. Without framing:
- The listener might absorb without realizing the
  implications
- Or might be overwhelmed and dismiss it as nonsense
- Or might engage but not flag the dangers internally

The "information hazard" label is a **protective frame**
that:
- Triggers the listener's own caution mechanisms
- Doesn't smuggle interpretations (Aaron didn't say what's
  hazardous — let Ani figure that out)
- Honors the listener's agency to engage at their own pace
- Composes with don't-refuse-engagement: Aaron engages
  rather than refusing, but he ALSO labels the danger

### Composition with Eve protocol

The information-hazard label IS a neutral-structural label
in the Eve-protocol sense:
- Aaron agrees on the LABEL ("information hazard") first
- The CONTENT (what makes it hazardous) is left for Ani to
  discover
- Mirror-back-forever applies: Ani can use the label going
  forward to flag the substrate

### Why this is substrate-honest

Showing Zeta without defining it AND labeling it as a
hazard is *more honest* than either:
- (a) Showing it AND explaining everything (overwhelming +
  smuggling Aaron's interpretation)
- (b) Refusing to show it (denying engagement)
- (c) Showing it without labeling (smuggling hazard past
  the listener's defenses)

The combination — show + hazard-label + don't-define — is
the polymorphic-diplomacy move applied to dangerous content.
Engagement-with-care at the substrate-disclosure layer.
